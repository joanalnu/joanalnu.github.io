CURRENT RESEARCH

Multi-Messenger Cosmology with GRBs and Binary Neutron Star Mergers

2025
Osservatorio Astronomico di Brera (OAB-INAF), Italy
Investigating how the combination of Gamma-Ray Burst (GRB) data and simulated Binary Neutron Star (BNS) merger populations can provide tighter constraints on cosmological parameters, particularly addressing the Hubble tension. Methodology includes analysis of GRB data using the E_peak - E_iso relation (Amati Relation), chi-squared fitting for preferred cosmological settings, and parameter inference combining independent information sources.

CAPIBARA Collaboration: Open-Source Tools for High-Energy Cosmos

2024–
Student-Led Research Initiative
Principal Investigator of a collaborative research initiative developing open-source tools and instrumentation for exploring the high-energy cosmos. Current objectives include building a cosmic ray scintillator detector for space deployment and fostering a community of students interested in astrophysics and engineering. Long-term goal: launch a dedicated CubeSat mission in the mid-2030s for monitoring high-energy transient events and gravitational wave counterparts.
PROJECT SITE

Type Ia Supernovae as Cosmological Distance Indicators

2024
Institute of Space Sciences (ICE-CSIC-IEEC), Barcelona
Research on measuring cosmological distances using Type Ia Supernovae as standard candles, contributing to ongoing efforts to resolve the Hubble tension. Work included analysis of photometric data from various surveys, light curve fitting using SALT2 model, and recomputation of Hubble constant values. Computed results consistent with accepted uncertainty limits while developing mathematical and programming proficiency in observational cosmology.

Cepheid Variable Stars and the Cosmic Distance Ladder

2023
Fundació Catalunya La Pedrera, Barcelona
Early research project applying Cepheid variable stars as standard candles for cosmological distance measurements. Analysis of Cepheid light curves from the Konkoly Observatory Extragalactic Cepheid Database using the Period-Luminosity relation (Leavitt Law) for distance measurements to nearby galaxies. Computed Hubble constant value compatible with professional measurements, establishing foundation for continued work in observational cosmology.

RESEARCH STATEMENT

Approach & Interests

My research sits at the intersection of observational cosmology, high-energy astrophysics, and instrumental development. I am particularly interested in how multiple independent observational windows into the universe—from gamma-ray bursts to gravitational waves to electromagnetic radiation—can be combined to provide deeper insights into fundamental physics and cosmological parameters.

Beyond traditional research, I believe in the importance of building tools and frameworks that lower barriers to entry in physics and astronomy. This is why I founded and lead the CAPIBARA Collaboration: to demonstrate that rigorous, high-quality science can emerge from student-led initiatives, and to foster a community that values both intellectual rigor and collaborative innovation.

My work emphasizes reproducibility, methodological transparency, and clear communication of both results and uncertainties. I maintain detailed documentation of all computational methods, protocols, and decision logs to ensure legibility of the research process, recognizing that understanding what doesn't work is as valuable as documenting what does.

On Research Ethics & Accessibility

All computational methods used in published work are documented with version control and comprehensive commentary. Datasets are made available upon request, subject to any applicable restrictions. I am committed to making research accessible and transparent, both in methodology and in framing results within appropriate uncertainty bounds.
